Reading Timer: 3:00
Reading Passage: "The Benefits of Team Work in Organizations"

In many organizations, teams are considered more effective than individuals working alone. There are several advantages to using teams in the workplace. First, teams are more efficient because members can divide tasks according to their expertise, allowing the group to complete projects faster than any single person could. Second, teams are more creative. When people from different backgrounds collaborate, they bring diverse perspectives that lead to innovative solutions. Brainstorming in a group often produces ideas that individuals would not think of on their own. Third, teams create accountability. When people work as part of a group, they are motivated to perform well because their contributions are visible to other team members. This social pressure encourages everyone to do their best work, resulting in higher quality output for the organization.

Lecture Transcript (Simulated)

Professor: Now, I know the reading passage makes teamwork sound wonderful, but research actually shows that teams are often less effective than individuals. Let me explain three reasons why.

First, teams often have "free riders" — people who don't contribute but still get credit for the group's work. This is a well-documented problem. When one or two members do most of the work while others coast along, the team is actually less efficient, not more.

Second, teams are usually slower than individuals. All those meetings, discussions, and the need to reach consensus take time. A single person can make a decision and act on it immediately. Teams have to coordinate schedules, debate options, and compromise — all of which slows things down considerably.

Third, influential team members can lead the group astray. Sometimes a dominant personality pushes the team toward a poor decision, and others go along with it to avoid conflict. This "groupthink" phenomenon means teams don't always produce better decisions — sometimes they produce worse ones because individuals suppress their own doubts.

Integrated Task — Model Essay (Score 5)
The reading passage argues that teamwork in organizations is beneficial because teams are more efficient, more creative, and create accountability. However, the lecturer refutes each of these points, arguing that teams are actually less effective than individuals. First, the reading states that teams are more efficient because members can divide tasks according to their expertise. In contrast, the lecturer points out that teams often have "free riders" who do not contribute but still receive credit. This means that one or two members end up doing most of the work, making the team less efficient, not more. Second, the reading claims that teams are more creative because diverse perspectives lead to innovative solutions. However, the lecturer argues that teams are usually slower than individuals because meetings, discussions, and the need to reach consensus take considerable time. A single person can make decisions and act immediately. Third, the reading asserts that teams create accountability through social pressure. Conversely, the lecturer warns that influential team members can lead groups astray. Dominant personalities may push teams toward poor decisions, and others may suppress their doubts to avoid conflict, a phenomenon known as groupthink. In summary, the lecturer systematically challenges each benefit described in the reading, suggesting that teamwork is often less effective than individual work.
Independent Task — Model Essay (Score 5)
I agree with the statement that technology has made communication between people better than it was in the past. While some argue that technology has reduced face-to-face interaction, I believe that the benefits of technological communication far outweigh its drawbacks. Technology has improved communication by making it faster, more accessible, and more inclusive. First, technology has made communication significantly faster. In the past, sending a message to someone in another country could take weeks through postal mail. Today, we can send an email or a text message instantly, and video calls allow us to speak face-to-face with people on the other side of the world in real time. This speed has transformed how we conduct business, maintain relationships, and share information. For example, when I was applying for jobs internationally, I was able to have video interviews with companies in three different countries within a single week — something that would have been impossible without modern technology. Second, technology has made communication more accessible to a wider range of people. Social media platforms, messaging apps, and email are available to anyone with a smartphone or internet connection. This accessibility means that people who might have been isolated in the past — such as those living in remote areas or individuals with mobility limitations — can now stay connected with friends, family, and colleagues. My grandmother, who lives in a rural village, uses video calls to see her grandchildren every week, which would not have been possible thirty years ago. Third, technology has made communication more inclusive by providing tools for people with different needs. Translation apps help people who speak different languages communicate with each other. Text-to-speech and speech-to-text technologies assist individuals with hearing or visual impairments. These tools were not widely available in the past, and their existence has opened up communication for millions of people who previously faced barriers. Some people argue that technology has made communication less personal and that people are more isolated despite being more connected. While it is true that excessive use of technology can lead to problems, this is a matter of how we use technology rather than a flaw of the technology itself. When used thoughtfully, technology enhances rather than replaces human connection. In conclusion, technology has made communication better by making it faster, more accessible, and more inclusive. The ability to connect instantly with anyone, anywhere, has transformed our personal and professional lives for the better. For these reasons, I firmly agree that technology has improved communication between people.
