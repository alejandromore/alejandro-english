TOEFL Speaking — Full Practice Section

Official Strategies & Rubrics · Based on The Official Guide 2021

📚 Speaking Strategies▼

Task 1 (Independent) — 15s prep, 45s response

State your preference clearly in the first sentence. Give 2 reasons with specific examples. Keep it simple and structured.

Task 2 (Integrated - Campus) — Read 45s + Listen + 30s prep + 60s response

State the change proposed in the reading. State the student's opinion. Give 2 reasons from the conversation.

Task 3 (Integrated - Academic) — Read 45s + Listen + 30s prep + 60s response

Define the concept from the reading. Give 2 examples from the lecture. Connect the examples back to the definition.

Task 4 (Integrated - Listen Only) — Listen + 20s prep + 60s response

Summarize the main points of the lecture. Include specific examples. Organize coherently with transitions.

General Tips

  • Do NOT memorize responses — speak naturally
  • Use transition words (first, second, for example, however)
  • Pace yourself — don't rush or speak too slowly
  • Speak clearly with good pronunciation and intonation
  • Use the full time allotted — don't finish too early
Task 1 · Independent

Independent Speaking

Prompt: Some people prefer to work from home, while others prefer to work in an office. Which do you prefer and why? Use specific reasons and examples to support your answer.
Preparation Time
15s prep → 45s response
💡 Model Answer▼

I prefer working from home because it gives me more flexibility and saves commuting time. First, I can organize my schedule around my most productive hours. For example, as a cloud architect, I often need to think deeply about system design, and being at home lets me focus without office distractions. Second, I save about two hours every day that I would otherwise spend commuting. This extra time allows me to learn new technologies and improve my skills. For these reasons, working from home is the better choice for me.

Task 2 · Integrated — Campus

Integrated Speaking — Campus Situation

Reading Passage (45 seconds to read):

Library Hours Extension
The university has announced that the main library will extend its operating hours from 10 PM to midnight, seven days a week, starting next semester. The change is intended to accommodate students who study late, particularly during exam periods. The university will hire additional security staff to ensure safety during the extended hours.
Conversation Transcript:

Man: Did you see the announcement about the library staying open until midnight?

Woman: Yes, and I think it's a great idea! I'm always studying late, especially during finals week. Last semester I had to leave the library at 10 PM right when I was in the middle of a project. Now I can stay two extra hours.

Man: But won't it cost a lot to hire more security staff?

Woman: That's true, but I think it's worth it. The library is the best place to study — it's quiet and has all the resources I need. Plus, having security there makes it safer for everyone. It's a small price to pay for better study conditions.
Preparation Time
30s prep → 60s response
💡 Model Answer▼

The university plans to extend library hours from 10 PM to midnight, seven days a week, and will hire additional security staff. The woman in the conversation agrees with this change. First, she says she often needs to study late, especially during finals week, and previously had to leave the library at 10 PM in the middle of her work. Now she can stay two extra hours to finish her projects. Second, she mentions that while hiring security staff costs money, it makes the library safer for everyone, and the library is the best place to study because it's quiet and has all the resources she needs. She believes it's a small price to pay for better study conditions.

Task 3 · Integrated — Academic

Integrated Speaking — Academic Course

Reading Passage (45 seconds to read):

Opportunistic Behavior
In biology, opportunistic behavior refers to the tendency of certain organisms to take advantage of immediately available resources or conditions to survive and reproduce, without regard for long-term consequences. Opportunistic species often thrive in unstable or unpredictable environments where resources fluctuate. They typically reproduce quickly and in large numbers, allowing them to rapidly exploit favorable conditions when they arise.
Lecture Transcript:

Professor: Today we'll look at two examples of opportunistic behavior in animals. First, consider the locust. Locusts are normally solitary insects, but when conditions are right — plenty of food and the right temperature — they transform dramatically. They begin to swarm, reproducing rapidly and devouring all available vegetation. They don't consider the long-term effect on the ecosystem; they simply exploit the current abundance.

Professor: A second example is the dandelion. Dandelions produce hundreds of seeds that scatter in the wind. They don't invest energy in producing a few well-protected seeds like oak trees do. Instead, they spread as many seeds as possible, taking advantage of any open patch of soil. This strategy allows them to quickly colonize disturbed ground, like a freshly plowed field, before other plants can establish themselves.
Preparation Time
30s prep → 60s response
Model Answerv

The reading defines opportunistic behavior as the tendency of organisms to exploit immediately available resources without regard for long-term consequences. The professor gives two examples. First, locusts are normally solitary, but when food and temperature conditions are favorable, they swarm and reproduce rapidly, devouring all vegetation without considering the long-term impact on the ecosystem. Second, dandelions produce hundreds of lightweight seeds that scatter in the wind, unlike oak trees which invest in a few well-protected seeds. This allows dandelions to quickly colonize disturbed ground, such as a freshly plowed field, before competing plants can grow. Both examples illustrate how opportunistic species take advantage of immediate conditions to survive and reproduce.

Task 4 · Integrated — Listen Only

Integrated Speaking — Lecture Summary

Lecture Transcript:

Professor: Today we'll discuss the concept of cognitive load in psychology. Cognitive load refers to the amount of mental effort being used in working memory. When cognitive load is too high, learning and performance suffer. There are two main types I want to cover.

Professor: First, there's intrinsic cognitive load. This is the inherent difficulty of the task itself. For example, learning to solve a complex calculus problem has a high intrinsic load because the material is inherently complex. You can't reduce this without breaking the task into simpler parts.

Professor: Second, there's extraneous cognitive load. This is caused by how information is presented, not by the content itself. For instance, if a textbook has tiny font, poor organization, and distracting diagrams, the student uses mental energy just trying to decode the presentation. A well-designed textbook with clear layout reduces this extraneous load, freeing up mental resources for actual learning. Understanding both types helps educators design better learning experiences.
Preparation Time
20s prep → 60s response
Model Answerv

The professor discusses cognitive load, which is the amount of mental effort used in working memory. When cognitive load is too high, learning and performance suffer. The professor describes two types. First, intrinsic cognitive load is the inherent difficulty of the task. For example, solving a complex calculus problem has high intrinsic load because the material is naturally complex, and it can only be reduced by breaking the task into simpler parts. Second, extraneous cognitive load is caused by how information is presented. For instance, a poorly designed textbook with tiny fonts and distracting diagrams forces students to use mental energy decoding the presentation. A well-designed textbook with clear layout reduces this load, freeing mental resources for learning. Understanding both types helps educators create better learning experiences.
